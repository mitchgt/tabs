PART ONE: NICK DRAKE

Question: How would you speculate for reasons on A) the lack of recognition for Nick Drake during his lifetime, and B) his subsequent much broader appeal, if somewhat underground, since his death?

Joe Boyd: The reasons he wasn't successful during his lifetime were a combination of fairly simple things. First of all, he didn't build up a live following or tour. The example who I guess could be a kind of parallel to Nick in some ways and did do well [was] Leonard Cohen. His records were released in North America at the height of the boom in FM radio, when people were playing a lot of album tracks. He didn't tour either. He didn't perform until well after he had become a famous person.
    Because Nick's records weren't released in America until the early '70s, it was really down to England to make him a star. He fell, unfortunately, in the period between the demise of the pirates. Really, all you had was BBC Radio One, and there wasn't really much room for album tracks or for artists like Nick on radio in Britain. Eliminate live performances, radio exposure - I mean, there isn't a lot there to get what he did across, and I think that...there were people in America. David Geffen was a big fan of his and was interested in releasing his records, [which] came out on Island in America, which really wasn't very well organized at that point in America.
    But at the same time, it's also true that I think the music doesn't reach out and grab people by the lapels. It takes a bit of getting used to, and it's also very English. I think for America at that time, a kind of unassertive introspective English musician wasn't necessarily going to get a lot of attention.
    That's the reasons he wasn't successful at the time. The reason why it's successful now is because - it's very simple - it's too good not to be. I think that his songs...there's a lot of wonderful stuff from that period in history, but a lot of things which were successful have a resonance because they remind people of a soundtrack to a kind of memory. "Sgt. Pepper's" is a timeless record, but there's a lot of records from that period which you can't reassess in a way objectively because they become so much of a part of the whole culture's memory of the '60s. But there are a lot of things which feel like they're a part of their time, and they have a fascination for that reason.
    But I think Nick's music doesn't really feel that way. It feels kind of outside of time in a way, and so it doesn't date. I think that his originality and intelligence of what he did...people, once they sit down quietly and listen to a Nick Drake record, very rarely lose interest after that.
    You hear people say how much they loved Nick Drake, [yet] his songs were very rarely covered, which I think is partly due to his uniqueness and the fact that what he did was...you can hear little echoes of other things that were going on at the time. But really, it was completely outside all trends and all movements. I think people are influenced by him in a more abstract way.

Question: Looking at his three albums, they're quite different from each other. Do you have a favorite phase of his development, or do you rate one of his approaches more highly than the other?

Joe Boyd: I like "Bryter Later" as album a lot. It's hard for me to be objective because I was involved as a producer on the first two, and I think "Bryter Later" is the most successful production. It's one of those albums that I can listen to without ever thinking "I should have done this better." I enjoy it every time I hear it. I would say that "Bryter Later" is my favorite.

Question: Nick has the image of being an extremely introverted person. Some comments I've read indicated that in the studio, if he was going to be expressive anywhere about his art or what he wanted to, that's when he was. Did you find that to be true?

Joe Boyd: He was expressive, but very quietly, and very shyly. He'd never behave as what you would call an extrovert. Sometimes you'd do something in the studio and I'd sort of look around and see what Nick thought of it and what somebody was doing as an overdub or something like that. He'd be looking at his shoes, he'd kind of stutter a couple of times, 'Well, uh, I'm not sure I like this." He was very clear, but the texture of the behavior was never extroverted. It was always very quiet and sort of self-contained, hesitant about voicing.

Question: Had Nick been able to have a long career, do you have any idea of how his music might have developed?

Joe Boyd: It's impossible to say, but I think when you listen to the guitar playing on the records, and put aside the singing, lyrics, and everything else, the arrangements, and you just listen to the guitar playing, you can hear that Nick was an extraordinary musician with very, very strong technique, big strong hands, the guitar playing was incredibly clean and accurate and inventive. The way he developed his tunings, some people still haven't figured out some of his tunes. I think he would have continued to develop as...not an eccentric person who happened to catch a wave at a certain time creatively and come up with some interesting stuff. He was a really deeply rooted musician with a lot of technique and a lot of awareness and intelligence and skill. I think he could have gone wherever he wanted to, really.

Question: How did those Elton John demos of his songs come about? [Elton John demoed some Drake compositions; those performances have since circulated on bootlegs.]

Joe Boyd: I hired him to do it. We did a session with Linda Thompson and Elton John. She sang half the songs and he sang half the songs. Some of the songs were Nick's, some were John Martyn's, some were Mike Heron's, Sandy Denny, Richard Thompson, all our writers were represented at that session.




PART TWO: SANDY DENNY

Question: With Fairport Convention, you were in the position to observe the band with both Judy Dyble and Sandy Denny. How do you feel the band changed? What did she add that hadn't been there before?

Joe Boyd: I think Sandy was a completely different person than Judy, a much more powerful singer, a much stronger voice for a start. So you had something much more - I think the band became less tentative with Sandy, because Judy was a delicate singer. I think there might have been a little bit of a feeling of holding back or hesitancy about being too aggressive in the band because the vocalist was so tentative, frail.
    But with Sandy, you had a powerhouse. I think she boosted the band's confidence in a way because she was quite well known as a solo artist. The fact that she chose to join Fairport I think boosted all of their confidence level in themselves as a band. And then she brought her own songwriting, great songs, and then ultimately I think she was really the key person ultimately responsible for introducing them to English folk music, because she had a large repertoire of songs that she used to sing with the band on the road or in hotel rooms after concerts and things.

Question: In their earlier recordings, there's much more of an emphasis on folk-rock as it was played by [US] West Coast groups of the time, and then by "Liege and Lief" the emphasis had shifted very much toward English traditional material. How would you compare the eras?

Joe Boyd: The defining event which led to the "Liege and Lief" period, which made Fairport what it has come to be ever since, was the car accident, where Martin Lamble was killed. The group at that time at first was very unsure that they would play together again. When they began to consider the possibility of reforming, they were very, very clear that they would never perform the old songs that Martin had played on. They would have to start from scratch with repertoire. They wouldn't go back and try to redo things they'd done with Martin. That led to the question, what will we do, what kind of repertoire will we do. I think that they had been very very...that all happened around the time [the Band's] "Music From Big Pink" came out, that was a big influence on them. They were very stunned by that record. I think they felt inspired by it to...it was so American, so rooted in American traditions that they felt that if they could come up with a kind of music which was as English as that record was American, that it would justify reforming as a group.
    It had already begun to be interesting during "Unhalfbricking" with "A Sailor's Life," but it hadn't really occurred to them to just go completely over to that other direction. That was the thing that triggered that change.

Question: How would you rate her work subsequent to Fairport?

Joe Boyd: She made some great records. It's a little hard for me to be objective, because I produced the ones with Fairport and [not] the subsequent ones. I wasn't too fond of Fotheringay as a band. I felt they had strengths and weaknesses. I think I didn't always agree with Trevor Lucas' choices as a producer on her subsequent albums. But there are fantastic tracks - "Solo," "My Donkey Ride" - from her solo albums, absolutely stunning tracks. I think she probably developed, improved as a songwriter. She got off to a very good start as a songwriter by writing "Who Knows Where the Time Goes" as her first song. But then you don't really think of fantastic songs of hers, memorable songs she wrote during the years she was with Fairport. She collaborated on a couple of good ones, she obviously sang fantastically well. But it wasn't, I think, until she left Fairport, in a way, that she started coming into her own as a songwriter on things like "The Sea" (with Fotheringay). It's just a fantastic body of work, most of which as a songwriter comes after she left Fairport.

Question: In Fairport, there were a few songwriters, which meant they might not have had the chance to record all of their material until they meant solo?

Joe Boyd: I suppose. The group was more important than the individuals.

Question: How have you heard Sandy's influence subsequent to her career?

Joe Boyd: Every time you hear a woman singing folk...Like anything innovative, they opened up a lot of Pandora's boxes. What they did was so strong that it's impossible in a way for a European rock band, Northern European rock band, to address its own traditional music in a rock context without quoting, in some way, from Fairport. Usually I find it depressing to listen to because the excesses of...to me, it's a kind of pale copy of what Fairport were doing. The same thing goes for women singers singing traditional music, particularly of the British Isles, in a modern context. You hear echoes of Sandy in an awful lot of contemporary female singers who have a feeling for tradition of some kind.




PART THREE: SYD BARRETT

Question: When you worked with the Pink Floyd, how did you view Syd's behavior changing during that time?

Joe Boyd: During the period I was really working with them, I never saw particular problems. I guess the period that I was in close contact with the group would be not that long a period, from October '66 until February/March of '67, a six-month period. And during that period I got to know them, worked on trying to get them a deal, presented them at UFO, and recorded three tracks in the studio with them.
    And all during that period, Syd was a delight. He was absolutely fine to work with, he was a little spacy but not much, he was witty and funny and energetic, and really the source for all the band material and the kind of centerpiece of the band. Roger [Waters] was a kind of anchor, the one who would organize the group's thoughts, in a way. But Syd was the spark. That was true very consistently from the time I first met them right through March, when we did "Interstellar Overdrive."
    And then, really, after that, I saw them only a few more times. The most memorable time being...They played four or five times at UFO in the early period, and then the single came out and the price went up and we couldn't afford them and there was some sort of...they were a little bit guilty because I was sort of losing them on two fronts, as a producer and [at the UFO]. Everybody knew they were just going to get bigger and bigger and bigger. So I asked, and they agreed, to come back and play the UFO three months later [for] their last gig at UFO.
    By June, when they came back to play the UFO, they were huge, and it was just a mob scene. That was where, I've told the story before, but...it was absolutely packed. You couldn't move. The band kind of elbowed their way through the crowd, and I had to sort of push them in the backs to make room for them to get in because there was no backstage entrance. And that was when I saw Syd for the first time in three months. I guess I'd seen him from a distance and people had told me that he'd taken a LOT of acid. He looked completely changed, he just looked like there was nobody home. He just stopped playing on stage.

Question: The recordings that [subsequent Pink Floyd producer] Norman Smith made: would there be any way you would have recorded the material differently?

Joe Boyd: I don't think so. I think "Piper at the Gates of Dawn" was a very good record. I think "Bike" is the best track they ever did. So even though I was pissed off at the time, I was very gratified when the band had to come back to Sound Techniques and use my engineer to get the sound they wanted for "See Emily Play." I was expecting the LP, when they finally produced it, to be something that I would not like or something. In fact, I thought it was pretty good. It's very hard to say.

Question: What did you think the group lost and gained when Syd left?

Joe Boyd: Syd wrote three-minute pop songs. Slightly weird pop songs, but they were three-minute compositions. The group, in live performance, would also do these long instrumentals, and they would extend songs of Syd's by these long solo sections. I've heard them doing a ten-minute version of "Arnold Layne." But still there was a structure, and a song. When Syd stopped being the man who wrote all their material, the whole structure of things changed. They became much more of an instrumental group, with the lyrics and the singing as something...it wasn't separate, but like an introduction to the instrumental, a jumping off point place for an instrumental work. I think Roger as a songwriter, and Dave Gilmour for that matter, both...they don't have the wit that Syd had. Syd was a very funny and inventive songwriter, and they don't really have that. But they do have something that probably may be more commercial than what Syd had, a kind of particular almost Wagnerian chord progressions that give their instrumental stuff a unique sound that was present in some of Syd's songs. "Interstellar Overdrive" is much more, in a way, a signpost forward to what they became, [than] the kind of music he wrote, which is...you can tell a Pink Floyd chord progression a mile away, there's something about it which is absolutely characteristic of them. The fact that it was primarily instrumental is one of the reasons why they can be as famous in Malaya and Brazil and Russia as they were in the United Kingdom and the United States.
    Syd and the Pink Floyd, that's one of the extraordinary things about them...there are very few imitators. Pink Floyd kind of exist somewhere on their own.

Question: What do you think of Syd's solo material?

Joe Boyd: I find it a little difficult to listen to because I remember Syd as a much more...as a better songwriter, I suppose. It was a bit painful in a way, it seemed...there were some good songs, as I recall, but it was a curiosity in a way, not something you held up as being great work by a great songwriter, it was kind of..."This is Syd Barrett, he used to be in Pink Floyd."
    One of the great sorrows in my collection is that I don't have the demo tape that Syd gave me of like six or eight songs that he hadn't recorded. I was recording a band called the Purple Gang and we were looking for material, and Syd gave us this tape. There were some terrific songs, very different from [what's on his solo LP] "The Madcap Laughs." Melodic, strong melodic good songs.
