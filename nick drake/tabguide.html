A QUICK GUIDE TO PLAYING TAB

Tablature (or tab for short) is a way of showing what happens
on each string at each point in time. So the six horizontal lines
each represent one string (lowest at the bottom, and with tunings
as given at the beginning of the tab piece), the numbers tell you
which fret is played, and moving left to right along the tab is
just like moving through a bar of music.

Incidentally, in some tab music the gaps between notes are
intended as rough guides only; I've tried as far as possible in
these Nick Drake tabs to make them completely accurate (so you
can literally count the dashes between notes as a measure of the
timing).


Some examples (in standard tuning):

|----
|----
|0-2-   - play the open G string, followed by the G string
|----     at the second fret (an A note)
|----
|----    

|--0-3---
|--1---1-     play a standard C chord,
|--0-----   - then a G note on the top string,
|--2-----     then a C on the B string and low C on 
|--3---3-     the A string at the same time.
|--------     

|--------|--------
|----1---|----1---  - couple of open A's, an A minor chord, then
|----2---|----2---    an open A and a quick E on the D string....
|----2--2|----2--2    then repeat. You might recognise the start
|0-0---0-|0-0---0-    of "Things Behind The Sun"!
|--------|--------


note that generally no hint is given as to left or right hand
fingering, (you just have to figure out for yourself which
fingers go where or pluck which string)), although in some
of the trickier tabs I've made suggestions for the right hand
(t-thumb, i-index, m-middle, r-ring, l-little).
also, no clue is given in these tabs (or many others) as to
whether notes hang across the gaps or are stopped - it's assumed
you know the piece of music well enough to imitate that yourself.

Chris Healey
