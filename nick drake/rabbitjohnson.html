There are basically two different approaches to choose from when recording a cover version. The first one is the attempt to copy the original as faithful as possible, and the second one is the personal interpretation. The latter approach is the most dangerous since it often carries the song too far away from its original style and intention (Nikki Sudden's rockabilly version of TIME HAS TOLD ME is the most horrifying example I've heard so far). The first approach is safer (although difficult enough in Nick Drake's case), but the result is destined to be more or less uninteresting.

A combination of these two approaches has often proved to work best. The Walkabouts' electric CELLO SONG, Shelleyan Orphan's piano-based JOEY, Scott Appel's atmospheric PLACE TO BE and Peter Bruntnell's uncompromising rendition of BLACK EYED DOG are all examples of interpretations which successfully combines originality with respect for the original versions.

English band The Rabbit's Hat make use of all these various approaches on their tribute album FLESH & NAIL. Players Steve Ellis (vocals, guitar, keyboards, banjo), Terri B (vocals, glockenspiel), Hodge (percussion) and Tim Jones (engineeering) possess the required musicality as well as an ability to make something fresh of Drake's songs - in this case the freshness is due to the subtle arrangements and the overall jazzy feel. Every track on this album is enjoyable, but the real gem here is their version of STRANGE MEETING II, an effortless treatment of light melancholia, which I'm sure Nick himself would have approved of.

Atlanta-based singer & songwriter Victor Johnson's first solo cd, TANGLED, is dedicated to the memory of Nick Drake. Drake's influence is primarily spotted in Johnson's nature-oriented and melancholic lyrics, while his singing voice might be likened to an American version of a classical trained Morrissey. Add David Sylvianesque song-titles like BENEATH THE SILVER MOON and we are bound to have high expectations. The result is not disappointing; the beautiful harmonies, the unexpected chord progressions and the inspired lyrics live up to every part of the description. For the next album, I would personally welcome some more "rough edges" in the deliverance and more variation in the arrangements, but it's nevertheless a promising debut.
